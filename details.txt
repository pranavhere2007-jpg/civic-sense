CivicSense: End-to-End Architecture and Platform Workflow

1. ZERO-EFFORT REPORTING & AI TRIAGE
The platform utilizes a "Zero-Effort Reporting" philosophy. To remove friction, users do not fill out manual forms. They simply take a photo of a civic issue and allow the system's location services to capture their exact GPS coordinates. 

Technical Implementation:
The image is converted to Base64 and sent to the Google Gemini 2.5 Flash Multimodal LLM. We utilize "Structured Outputs" (responseSchema) to mathematically force the AI to return a strict JSON object instead of conversational text. The AI acts as a triage inspector, auto-generating the title, description, category, and a "scaleScore" (1 to 10).

The NGO Logic Gate:
If the AI assigns a scaleScore greater than 6, or categorizes the issue as heavy "Infrastructure," a boolean flag (requiresNGO) is set to true. This decision was made to protect everyday citizens from legal liability and physical danger. Massive issues (like collapsed roads or live power lines) require heavy machinery and government permits. These high-scale issues are stored in the Firebase NoSQL database but are locked on the frontend, visually displaying as "Dispatched to Verified Agencies." Normal citizens can only claim safe, low-scale issues.

2. CLAIMING & THE PLAN OF ACTION
When a user decides to fix an open issue, they must click "Volunteer to Fix" and submit a written "Plan of Action." 

Reasoning:
This acts as a psychological commitment mechanism to prevent users from accidentally claiming issues or trolling the map. Once claimed, the database updates the issue status, assigning the user's unique Firebase Auth UID to the task. The volunteer is placed on a 24-hour Service Level Agreement (SLA) to submit their first piece of visual proof (the process photo).

3. THE ZERO-TRUST VERIFICATION ENGINE
The core of the application is the Zero-Trust Verification pipeline. Because the system features gamification (awarding points for resolving issues), we cannot simply trust that a user uploaded a valid picture. 

Step A: The Geospatial Net (Haversine Formula)
Before an image is even uploaded to our Cloudinary storage, the frontend captures the user's live GPS coordinates. It runs the Haversine formula—a mathematical equation that calculates the exact distance between the user's current location and the original issue's coordinates across the curvature of the Earth. If the user is more than 50 meters away, the upload is hard-rejected locally. This prevents "couch-volunteering."

Step B: The Environmental & Contextual AI
If the user passes the GPS check, the original issue photo and the new process/final photo are sent to the Gemini AI. 
- For Process Photos: The AI is instructed to strictly compare the background environments (pavement, walls, trees) to ensure the user is at the exact right spot. It ignores resolution status.
- For Final Photos: The AI checks the background AND evaluates if the specific civic issue (e.g., the garbage pile) has been visibly removed or repaired. 
If the AI detects a mismatch, the image is rejected, saving database storage space and preventing fraud.

4. DECENTRALIZED CONSENSUS (THE COMMUNITY JURY)
Once the AI approves a final photo, the system does not immediately close the issue. It shifts the status to "Pending Verification." 

Reasoning & Implementation:
Relying entirely on the original reporter to verify a fix creates a bottleneck (they might delete the app or never log back in). Instead, we built a localized Community Jury. 

The app queries the Firebase database for all "Pending Verification" issues and runs the Haversine formula again to display only the issues within a 5-kilometer radius of the current user. Locals can view the Before and After photos and vote to approve or reject the fix. 

This introduces a secondary gamification loop: users earn points (5 pts) just for participating in the jury. Once an issue receives 3 upvotes, the decentralized consensus is reached. The database flips the status to "Resolved," the original volunteer is awarded their full points (50 pts), and the issue is dynamically filtered off the live map to prevent clutter. If rejected, it reverts to "In Progress," forcing the volunteer to finish the job.
